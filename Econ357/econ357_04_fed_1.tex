\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{1. History prior to 1960}
    \framesubtitle{A. 19th Century}

    In the early 1800s there were two separate iterations of a Central Bank.   Both times the bank was allowed to lapse, the second by Andrew Jackson in 1836.
    \vspace{1em}

    Absence of a central bank led to a chaotic financial system:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item In some periods there was too much money, which led to inflation and eventually a crash (e.g. 1840s)
        \item In other periods, there was not enough money, which led to deflation and depression (e.g. William Jennings Bryan crusade for Silver in the 1890s)
    \end{itemize}

    Even so, here was fierce resistance to reestablishing a central bank:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Fear of centralized power
        \item Distrust of moneyed interests
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

    \framesubtitle{B. Panic of 1907}

    \begin{columns}
    
    \begin{column}{0.5\textwidth}
        \footnotesize
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Started with an attempted “short squeeze”, financed by borrowing.   The holders of the short positions withstood, and the resulting losses on the leveraged positions led to a bank run and eventual failure of NY’s 3rd largest bank.
            \item Contagion spread and interest rates between banks reached 70\%.
            \item Eventually stopped when JP Morgan organized a group financiers to support NY’s banks and stock exchange
        \end{itemize}
    \end{column}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{1. History prior to 1960}
    \framesubtitle{B. Creation of the Federal Reserve}

    \begin{itemize}
        \item The Panic of 1907 led bankers and legislators to prioritize financial reform, and eventually led to the Federal Reserve Act of 1913.
        \item The Fed was initially conceived to, in part, stabilize the financial system and stop banking panics.
        \item The Fed’s function was modeled after European central banks.   It was intended to be “lender of last resort” (the role that JP Morgan played in 1907).
        \item Bagehot’s dictum (1873): “Central banks should early and lend freely, against good collateral, and at high rates”
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{1. History prior to 1960}
    \framesubtitle{B. Creation of the Federal Reserve}

    \textbf{Federal Reserve Act of 1913:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Authority shared between the Board, located in DC, and twelve Regional Reserve Banks located throughout the country, mostly towards the East.
        \item Elaborate System of Checks and Balances, including that each Regional Bank was a quasi-public institution owned by private commercial banks in the district that are members of the Fed system
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{1. History prior to 1960}
    \framesubtitle{D. WWII}

    \textbf{Fed-Treasury Accord of 1951:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The Fed ended its policy of capping government bond yields.
        \item The Fed-Treasury Accord removed the Treasury Secretary from the FOMC.  (Prior to 1951 the Treasury Secretary had been part of the Federal Open Market Committee)
        \item Started the period of Federal Reserve independence
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{Summary of History prior to 1960}

    \begin{itemize}
        \item The Fed was created in 1913, as a response to the Panic of 1907.   The Fed was initially created to stabilize the financial system and to be a “lender of last resort.”
        \item The Fed contributed to the severity of the Great Depression by allowing the money supply to contract.   Since then the Fed has adopted a more active approach in managing economic outcomes and the money supply.
        \item The Fed supported the government in WWII by buying US Treasury bonds.   While effective during the war, this policy contributed to post-war inflation.   The Fed has since stopped explicitly coordinating with the Treasury Department.

    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{4. Structure of the Federal Reserve}
    \framesubtitle{Board of Governors}

    \begin{itemize}
        \item Seven members headquartered in Washington, D.C.
        \item Appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate
        \item 14-year non renewable term
        \item Required to come from different districts
        \item Very large and experienced research staff located in DC.  Often sets the forecast and the agenda for the FOMC.
    
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{4. Structure of the Federal Reserve}
    \framesubtitle{Board of Governors}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Fed Chair is appointed by the President.   Needs to be confirmed by the Senate.
    \item Fed Chairs are appointed to four year terms.
    \item Fed Chairs terms are often renewed.   Many recent Fed Chairs have served two consecutive terms (8 years total).
    \item The President also appoints a Vice Chair.   Often chosen to be a complement to the Fed Chair.
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{4. Structure of the Federal Reserve}
    \framesubtitle{New York Federal Reserve}

    The Federal Reserve Bank of New York plays a special role in the Federal Reserve System for several reasons.

    \begin{itemize}
        \item First, its district contains many of the largest commercial banks in the United States, the safety and soundness of which are paramount to the health of the U.S. financial system.
        \item The second reason for the New York Fed’s special role is its active involvement in the bond and foreign exchange markets.
        \item President of New York is a permanent voting member of the FOMC
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{4. Structure of the Federal Reserve}
    \framesubtitle{Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC)}

The main policy setting body is the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Meets eight times a year
    \item Consists of seven members of the Board of Governors, the president of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, and the presidents of four other regional banks.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The regional bank presidents rotate.
        \item In 2026 the presidents of the Cleveland, Minneapolis, Dallas, and Philadelphia banks are members of the FOMC.
    \end{itemize}
    \item Chairman of the Board of Governors is also chair of FOMC.
    \item Issues directives to the trading desk at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York
\end{itemize}



\end{frame}
