\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{1. Motivation: The Zero Lower Bound}
    \framesubtitle{}

The reason that yields at different maturities have different yields include:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Expectations Hypothesis}: Investors expect future short rates to be different than current short rates
    \item \textbf{Market Segmentation Hypothesis}: Bonds at different maturities are NOT substitutes, there may be reasons why investors prefer certain maturities, which leads to an increase in the price of those bonds.
    \item \textbf{Liquidity/Risk Premium}: Longer maturity bonds are riskier, and investors may require a price discount (higher yields) to be incentivized to hold riskier bonds
\end{enumerate}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{1. Motivation: The Zero Lower Bound}
    \framesubtitle{}

    Lower Interest Rates “ease” Policy 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Encourages borrowing
        \item Can raise asset prices by lowering discount rates
        \item Looser financial conditions
        \item Lower returns on safe assets pushes investors towards riskier assets, which in turn makes financing easier for riskier ventures
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{1em}

    Note that most of these operate through longer maturity interest rates.   For example, when you buy a house, the mortgage rate is usually based on 7 year maturity bonds.
    \vspace{1em}
    
    \textit{Given that the Fed controls only overnight interest rates, how could they bring down longer dated interest rates?}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{2. Interest Rate Policy}
    \framesubtitle{Negative Interest Rates: Real Rates}

    Real interest rates have often been negative.  According to the Fisher Equation, this can happen if inflation is high and the nominal interest rate is low.     
    \vspace{1em}
    
    \textbf{Fisher Equation}: 
    
    $$\text{Nominal Interest Rate} = \text{Real Interest Rate} + \text{Inflation Rate}$$
    \vspace{1em}
    
    Also, nothing prevents longer term bond yields from being negative.   Price and yields are inversely related.   If prices move high enough (because there is a lot of demand) then yields will move negative.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{2. Interest Rate Policy}
    \framesubtitle{Forward Guidance}

    In some circumstances the Fed may attempt to influence expectations for future short rates through its communication policy.  The idea is to lower yields at longer maturity through the expectations channel.  This is known as “forward guidance”.   
    \vspace{1em}
    
    Any communications policy faces the challenge of being credible.   The Fed could, in theory, say one thing now, but do something different later.
    \vspace{1em}

    
    The Fed has attempted a few different things to make its forward guidance more credible:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Qualitative
        \item Date based
        \item Outcome based
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{2. Interest Rate Policy}
    \framesubtitle{Forward Guidance: Qualitative}

    In March 2009 the Fed provided \textbf{qualitative} forward guidance.
    \vspace{1em}

    \begin{quote}
     “The Committee will maintain the target range for the federal funds rate at 0 to 1/4 percent and anticipates that economic conditions are likely to warrant exceptionally low levels of the federal funds rate for an extended period”
    \end{quote}
    \vspace{1em}

    Note especially the phrase \textit{"for an extended period"}.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{2. Interest Rate Policy}
    \framesubtitle{Forward Guidance: Date-Based}

    In March 2011 the Fed provided \textbf{date-based} forward guidance.
    \vspace{1em}

    \begin{quote}
     "To promote the ongoing economic recovery and to help ensure that inflation, over time, is at levels consistent with its mandate, the Committee decided today to keep the target range for the federal funds rate at 0 to 1/4 percent.  The Committee currently anticipates that economic conditions--including low rates of resource utilization and a subdued outlook for inflation over the medium run--are likely to warrant exceptionally low levels for the federal funds rate at least through mid-2013"
    \end{quote}
    \vspace{1em}

    Note especially the phrase \textit{"at least through mid-2013"}.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{2. Interest Rate Policy}
    \framesubtitle{Forward Guidance: Outcome-Based}

    In December 2012 the Fed provided \textbf{outcome-based} forward guidance.
    \vspace{1em}

    \begin{quote}
    December 2012: “In particular, the Committee .... currently anticipates that this exceptionally low range for the federal funds rate will be appropriate at least as long as the unemployment rate remains above 6-1/2 percent, inflation between one and two years ahead is projected to be no more than a half percentage point above the Committee’s 2 percent longer-run goal, and longer-term inflation expectations continue to be well anchored.”
    \end{quote}
    \vspace{1em}

    Note the numeric targets for unemployment and inflation.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{2. Interest Rate Policy}
    \framesubtitle{Forward Guidance: Considerations}

    Considerations:
    \vspace{1em}
    
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The guidance may be counter-productive if it conveys a discouraging message about the Fed’s expectations for future growth and inflation
        \item Does the guidance provide any new information?
        \item Is the guidance a commitment?  What if the world changes?
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{3. Balance Sheet Policy}
    \framesubtitle{}

    \begin{itemize}
        \item In a previous class we discussed how the Fed controls the money supply by creating reserves (reserves are liabilities for the Fed, assets for the banking system).   By controlling the money supply, the Fed indirectly affects bond yields (see the Market for Money model).
        \item The Fed may also be able to influence bond yields through the bonds that it purchases.   Specifically, if the Fed purchases enough bonds, it will increase the total demand for bonds, and drive prices higher (yield lower)
        \item The Fed can choose which bonds to buy, and therefore which parts of the yield curve to move.
        \item Using bond purchases to increase bond prices and thereby lower bond yields has been referred to as \textbf{quantitative easing}.
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
    \frametitle{3. Balance Sheet Policy}
    \framesubtitle{Operation Twist}

In 2011 the Fed started \textbf{Operation Twist}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sell} short dated US Treasuries 
    \item \textbf{Buy} long dated US Treasuries 
\end{itemize}
\vspace{1em}

The intended effect was to raise the prices of long term bonds, which would bring \textbf{down} long term yields.  
\vspace{1em}

The risk that short term yields would rise was limited by the expectations hypothesis and forward guidance.
\vspace{1em}

\end{frame}
