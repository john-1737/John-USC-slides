\begin{frame}[t]
\frametitle{2. Bank Earnings Management}
\framesubtitle{Liquidity Management}

    Banks \textbf{must} provide cash when depositors demand it.
    \vspace{1em}

    Considerations:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Use reserves/cash (if available)
        \item Borrow an offsetting amount in public markets
        \item Borrow from the Fed (discount window)
        \item Sell securities
        \item Sell loans (most costly option, want to avoid this!)
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
\frametitle{2. Bank Earnings Management}
\framesubtitle{Asset Management: Credit Risk}

    Banks seek to minimize credit losses
    \vspace{1em}

    Considerations:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Asymmetric information:
        \item Before making a loan = adverse selection
        \item After making a loan = moral hazard
        \item Tools to address – screening / underwriting and monitoring
        \item Other: long term customer relationships, collateral and compensating balances, local knowledge
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
\frametitle{2. Bank Earnings Management}
\framesubtitle{Asset Management: Interest Rate Risk}

    Changes in interest rates causes changes in asset values and liability costs.
    \vspace{1em}


    Considerations:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Banks frequently engage in maturity transformation: borrow short (deposits) and lend long (loans)
        \item This creates an imbalance and a vulnerability.  If interest rates increase, assets may decline in price AND the cost of their liabilities may increase.
        \item “Duration” = changes in value due to changes in interest rates.   Banks can reduce the risk by matching the "duration" of assets and liabilities.
        \item Strategies: borrow long, lend short, interest rate swaps, 
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[t]
\frametitle{2. Bank Earnings Management}
\framesubtitle{Capital Management}

    Wells Fargo: 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item In 2016 Wells Fargo was found to have created a number of fake accounts for its clients.
        \item The Federal Reserve punished Wells Fargo by instituting a \$1.95 tr cap on assets.
        \item The asset cap indirectly reduced leverage (WF had to turn away deposits)
        \item WFC equity value was stagnant during this period.
        \item The Federal Reserve removed the asset cap in June 2025.
        \item WFC equity increased by 30\% in 2025
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{practiceframe}[t]
\frametitle{Practice}

Regulators attempt to reduce the riskiness of banks' asset portfolios by
\begin{enumerate}[A.]
    \item limiting the amount of loans in particular categories.
    \item encouraging banks to hold risky assets such as common stocks.
    \item establishing a minimum interest rate floor that banks can earn
    \item requiring collateral for all loans.
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{1em}

For a given return on assets, the lower is bank capital
\begin{enumerate}[A.]
    \item the lower is the return for the owners of the bank.
    \item the higher is the return for the owners of the bank.
    \item the lower is the credit risk for the owners of the bank.
    \item the lower the possibility of bank failure
\end{enumerate}


\end{practiceframe}


\begin{practiceframe}[t]
\frametitle{Practice}

When a \$10 check written on the First National Bank of Chicago is deposited in an account
at Citibank, then
\begin{enumerate}[A.]
    \item the liabilities of the First National Bank decrease by \$10.
    \item the reserves of the First National Bank increase by \$10.
    \item the liabilities of Citibank decrease by \$10.
    \item the assets of Citibank decrease by \$10.
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{1em}

Which of the following are reported as liabilities on a bank's balance sheet?
\begin{enumerate}[A. ]
    \item reserves
    \item checkable deposits
    \item consumer loans
    \item deposits with other banks
\end{enumerate}

\end{practiceframe}
